# Design Principles

Use this file after you know the audience, the deck type, and whether the artifact should feel more executive or more proof-driven.

## Core Principle

A good customer deck is not a poster and not a document.

It should:

- state one main idea per slide
- make the recommendation feel intentional
- show enough proof to support the meeting goal
- leave enough whitespace that the room can scan it quickly

## Visual Thesis Dimensions

Define the deck on these axes before drafting slides:

1. `Tone`
   - executive
   - consultative
   - technical
   - transformation-led
2. `Density`
   - minimal
   - balanced
   - proof-heavy
3. `Proof style`
   - cards
   - matrix
   - architecture
   - phased roadmap
   - value summary
4. `Rhythm`
   - statement -> proof -> recommendation -> next step
   - pressure -> platform thesis -> proof -> roadmap

## Typography Hierarchy

- Headline should carry the answer, not just the topic.
- Subtitle should add context, not instructions to the presenter.
- Card titles should be shorter and stronger than card bodies.
- Body copy should be quiet enough that the hierarchy is obvious at a glance.

If the headline wraps awkwardly, shorten it before shrinking it.

## Density Rules

- Prefer `1` headline, `1` hero device, and `1` interpretation per slide.
- Prefer `3-4` cards, not `6-8`.
- Prefer `3-5` lines per content box.
- For wide horizontal bars, write body copy as an intentional `1-2` lines instead of trusting automatic wrapping.
- In presenter-led decks, keep spoken interpretation, guardrails, and CTA nuance in notes before adding another visible bar to the canvas.
- Split a slide before shrinking body copy into clutter.
- Avoid two unrelated proof devices on the same slide.

## Spacing Rules

- Keep a stable outer margin across the deck.
- Keep visible gaps between sibling cards and containers.
- Keep text inset from shape borders.
- Do not let labels or notes drift into the header bar or footer area.
- Use footer-area summary bars sparingly. They are for audience-facing leave-behind content, not for presenter coaching that belongs in notes.
- On architecture slides, keep callout cards and captions off connector lanes.

## Palette Direction

- Use a restrained palette.
- One accent color and one secondary family is usually enough.
- Let emphasis come from hierarchy and whitespace first, not from many colors.
- When adapting Oracle-style material, preserve the calm enterprise feel rather than adding decorative novelty.

## Architecture Slide Treatment

Architecture slides inside a customer deck should:

- feel like part of the same presentation
- keep OCI icons and grouping honest
- use callouts to explain the takeaway instead of crowding the topology
- stay legible from the back of the room

Do not let the architecture slide become a second visual system with totally different spacing and typography behavior.

## What To Avoid

- visible presenter coaching
- overstuffed grids
- long paragraphs inside cards
- captions on top of connectors
- decorative shapes that do not help the story
- slides that read like raw export artifacts rather than presentation material
