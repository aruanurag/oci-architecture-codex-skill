# Why OCI North Star

Use this file when the user wants a customer-facing OCI deck that should feel like a polished Oracle field presentation without copying a specific internal deck.

## How To Use This Reference

- Prefer a user-provided, approved internal `Why OCI` starter deck when one exists.
- Treat any starter deck as a pattern library for structure, pacing, layout, and proof-slide choices.
- Do not copy or lightly paraphrase visible copy, charts, proof points, customer stories, or sourced claims from that deck unless the user separately provides them as approved facts to reuse.
- If no starter deck is available, build from scratch using the principles below.

## Narrative Pattern

The strongest `Why OCI`-style decks usually move in this order:

1. A cover slide with a clear value proposition or meeting outcome.
2. A `why now` or customer-pressure slide that makes the business context explicit.
3. A slide that explains what OCI does differently at the platform level.
4. `2-4` proof themes that matter for this customer, such as workload fit, economics, distributed cloud, security posture, database strategy, or AI.
5. A customer-specific recommendation or target-state view.
6. An adoption path, decision request, or next step.

Not every deck needs every stage. Keep only the parts that support the meeting objective.

## Slide Archetypes

Use a small set of repeatable slide types:

- `Statement slide`: one sharp takeaway headline with minimal supporting text.
- `Pressure slide`: customer pain, industry pressure, or decision criteria in a compact visual form.
- `Proof slide`: one hero chart, map, matrix, or capability visual with a short interpretation.
- `Pillar slide`: `3-4` reasons OCI fits this customer, each expressed in business language rather than service catalogs.
- `Recommendation slide`: explicit OCI approach, tradeoffs, and what happens next.

Avoid chaining too many proof slides without returning to the customer decision.

## Content Strategy

- Lead with the customer problem before describing OCI.
- Translate OCI into workload-specific value, such as faster delivery, lower operating risk, architectural flexibility, AI readiness, or simpler modernization.
- Use only proof categories that advance the ask. A short executive briefing rarely needs all of economics, AI, distributed cloud, security, and platform breadth.
- Keep one major idea per slide. If a slide is trying to prove two unrelated things, split it.
- Separate what the customer has said, what you know, what you infer, and what you recommend.

## Visual Strategy

- Use declarative, outcome-led headlines rather than topic labels.
- Favor generous whitespace and a single hero visual or proof device on each slide.
- Use short cards or callouts instead of dense paragraphs.
- Mix slide rhythms: statement, proof, recommendation, then next step. Avoid `8` consecutive slides with the same layout.
- Keep the palette restrained. Use deep neutrals or muted backgrounds with one or two accent colors instead of many competing colors.
- Use a clear typography hierarchy: strong headline treatment and quieter supporting text. If a starter deck already defines theme fonts, preserve the hierarchy rather than inventing a new one.

## What To Replace In A Starter Deck

When adapting a source deck, rewrite or remove:

- customer examples that do not belong to the target account
- statistics or benchmarks that are not validated for reuse
- charts whose labels or numbers are not approved
- internal speaker coaching or author prompts
- internal-only confidentiality marks or legal text that should not appear in the customer version

## Review Focus

Before finalizing, check:

- Does the deck feel customer-specific rather than like a generic Oracle corporate pitch?
- Does every slide have a visible takeaway?
- Is there a clear rhythm from challenge to proof to recommendation?
- Did any inherited text survive without a deliberate decision to keep it?
- Is the deck visually calmer and more spacious than a typical product dump?
